Le tavolette di Cnosso sono scritte in greco? - 1 Giugno 1952

In questa nota datata al 1 giugno 1952 Ventris si interroga sulla possibilità che le tavolette in Lineare B rappresentino un dialetto greco arcaico.

Con la pubblicazione di Scripta Minoa II di Arthur Evans con l'annesso indice di Bennett, è possibile avviare un'analisi dettagliata del materiale. Evans in questo studio sostiene che i Minoici non parlassero greco, mentre i Micenei sì.

Si ipotizza, pertanto, che la Lineare B possa rappresentare una forma primitiva di greco, forse adottata da una classe dirigente greca che si sarebbe stabilita a Cnosso intorno al 1450 a.C. Questa teoria, però, implica che molti toponimi e nomi personali nelle tavolette potrebbero essere rimasti indigeni, mescolati a termini greci. Un punto di partenza promettente per la decifrazione sono i cosiddetti “segni di categoria 3”, gruppi di simboli che sembrano riferirsi a liste di merci o risorse, spesso ripetuti in un ordine fisso. Alcuni di questi segni potrebbero indicare “città” o “corporazioni”, analogamente a quanto osservato negli archivi di Ugarit.

NOTE 20. Are the Knossos and Pylos tablets wrritten in Greek?

With the publication of the Knossos and Pylos tablets written in Scripto Minoa volume II, and with the promise of Dr. Bennett's revised Index, we are in a position to begin a detailed analysis of the whole Linear B material under its various aspects. One of the first studies of this Ktistopoulos' "Recherches sur les mots minoens", which I have just received. Until this work has been done, it will not to start as well not to start with any preconceived notions about phonetic values or about the language relationships. The Note which follows 18 in the nature of a frivolous digression, and is not intended to prejudice that analyals.

There will very likely turn out to be 2 or 3 different "points of departure" for a successful decipherment, possibly found by several of us (like the identifications of "king/Baal/house" and of the cardinal numbers in the Ugarit alphabet). In the chains of deduction whioh spread out from these points we may, I believe, initially strike words and forms which force us to ask ourselves whether we are not, after all, dealing with a Greek dialect. These may well turn out to be a hallucination; but let us discuss for a moment whether such a possibility is intrinsically abourd, or whether it is still worth bearing in mind in the analysis. Evans and many others have felt that the Knossians of LMII cannot have spoken Greek, because the whole appearance of their culture seems to preserve the continuity of the old island traditions; but they believed they could detect, in the culture of the Mainland, signs of differences which showed that the Myceneans already spoke Greek. Two facts complicate this picture:

  1. The language of all Mainland sites, including Pylos of about 1200 BC, is that of the Knossos Linear B texts.
  2. Bennett and others have thought that the language of Linear B is distinct from that Linear A, and that the new script was perhaps adapted specifically to cope with this new language.

If we were to toy with the idea of an early Greek dialect, we should have to assume that a Greek ruling class, appearences to contrary, established itself at Knossos as early as 1450, and that the new Linear B was adapted from the indigenous syllabary in order to write Greek. We might, however, assume that most of the placenames and personal names on the tablets are still indigenous, and that perfhaps some native words and phrases occur side by side with Greek ones.

One of the most promising "points of departure" concerns those sign-groups at Pylos and Knossos which I have classified as Category 3. They are the sign-groups which are not personal names, and yet figure as the subjects of very varied lists of commodities, often recurring in a fixed order and with a fixed proportion between their various contributions. Their commonest members are formed, in each case, by a group of about a dozen of them, which are found in a disproportionately large number of entries. They form an "adjectival" variant by changing their last syllabe to Vowel 3 and adding - , or - and their typical representatives are the Pylos "9" of Vn01 etc, and the Knossos sign-groups in - / - and / - which Dr.Kober discussed in 1946.

From the analogy of the contemporary accounts from Ras Shamra/Ugarit published by Virolleaud, which should be one of our most valuable aids, I think it is likely that the Category 3 sign-groups correspond to the “ towns and corporations” of Ugarit (SYRIA XXI, 1940, pp 123-151). Those which occur both at Pylos and at Knossos are probably “corporations”; those which are peculiar to each are the “towns” and villages of the region, the “adjectival” forms in - / - being their ethnica, corresponding to the Ugaritic forms in - y. To test this assumption, we must be able to identify some of the placenames which occur in the records; and the chances are much the best at Knossos, where a much greater number of the surrounding settlements can be identified by the names which they retained in classical times. We have gone some way towards sorting the syllabic signs into their right places on the Grid (Figure 11, Note 17), and there are three phonetic identifications which seem worth investigation: That -, because of its great initial frequency, represents , as Ktistopoulos has also suggested. That the series / / contain the consonant n, because of Cypriot in a, because n is a verbal ending in Etruscan (and on Lemnos?), and because of the adjectival use of -n- in Etruscan, especially after the forms of the genitive singular. That Vowel 1, including / etc, represents -i-, because of Cypriot / = ti, and because this vowel seems very common before - and never occurs before - ( -i ?).

One of the most likely Cretan placenames is Amnisos, a few kilometres NE of Knossos, the site of the cave of Eileithyia, and according to Strabo the port of Minos. Evans regarded the area immediately east of Iraklion as having been the Harbour Town, but Marinatos has revealed another town, and quayside, at Amnisos in his excavations from 1932 onwards, and he evidently believed he had uncovered what was the more important port of access to Knossos - the one, as he says, at which one may imagine Theseus to have landed and from which Idomeneus sailed. If Amnisos does occur among the Category 3 names, and the phonetic assumptions above are correct, we should expect it to be written in the form - - -; and we do in fact find that one common sign-group, and one only, contains these signs - / - /, which forms an adjectival form from / = - -, and a locative (?) / - / = -. The two syllables / = both contain Vowel 1, suitable to a “dead”-vowel sequence -mi-ni-, while / = containing Vowel 2, is perhaps -so-. If so, all the other placenames in - - evidently must represent placenames in - - -, - - as well. The other frequent member of this series is /, which on the same phonetic assumptions should read - - - -, both sharing Vowel 2. Now it is tempting to regard the consonant of / as k. One thing that influences me is the word on Pylos Kn02.2,(5),8,r3, which seems to refer to the head man of each / -, and would then give a k -, tying in with Etruscan words beginning in a-k-o- and containing the sense of “ownership” (of Lemnian a-k-e-ri). This would give us ko-no-so- - for /, showing the same kind of vowel-harmony in the first two signs and, if our phonetic assumptions are correct (which is very difficult to prove) quite evidently the Minoan spelling of Knossos.
